## Parry practice routine

**Matt:**
The low frustration thing sounds interesting.

**ChatGPT:**
Good choice. I'll keep this practical, bounded, and low-friction — not a "git gud" speech. Structure: design principles → concrete loops → how to know it's working → failure modes.

**1. Design principles for low-frustration parry learning**

- Constraint A: damage must not end the fight. If your damage is high enough to skip phases, you don't get repetitions. You want fights where you can choose not to end them quickly.
- Constraint B: mistakes must be survivable. Learning parry timing requires missing several in a row and regaining rhythm mid-fight. You want enemies whose punish damage is noticeable but not fight-ending.
- Constraint C: repetition beats variety. Parry skill is pattern recognition, not reflex. You want few moves, repeated often, with clear telegraphs.
- Constraint D: parry should be optional, not required. If the fight requires perfect parries, frustration spikes. Parry should be a bonus, not a survival gate.

**2. Three concrete low-frustration parry practice loops**

🔁 Loop 1 — "Deliberately drag out normal fights": Enter normal (non-Chromatic) encounters you already trivialise. Do not open with Fencer's Flurry or burst chains — instead use basic attacks, low-impact skills, and stance maintenance. You're practising parry timing with low stakes, predictable enemy patterns, and recovering rhythm after a miss. Key mental shift: "This fight is a practice dummy, not content."

🔁 Loop 2 — "One-enemy focus drills": Enter a multi-enemy fight. Kill all but one enemy quickly, then stop attacking aggressively, let the last enemy cycle attacks, and parry repeatedly. Single pattern, high repetition, zero chaos. Especially good for enemies with clear wind-ups.

🔁 Loop 3 — "Chromatic-lite training": Fight a Chromatic enemy you already know. Intentionally unequip one damage Lumina or delay Break intentionally. Goal is not to win — the goal is parry stamina: can you keep timing for 10+ turns? Important rule: if frustration rises, stop immediately. This loop is powerful but only when used sparingly.

**3. How to measure improvement (without winning fights)**

Good signs: you parry some attacks reliably (not all); you can recover after a miss; your success rate stabilises (e.g. ~60%) instead of oscillating; you start parrying earlier in the animation, not reactively.

Bad signs: you tense up and guess; you try to parry everything; you restart fights repeatedly.

**4. One technique that helps most people**

Watch the body, not the weapon. For most enemies, weapons have misleading arcs, effects add noise, and camera motion lies. Instead, watch hips / shoulders and parry on commitment, not contact. You already proved you can do this with Julien — that skill does transfer.

**5. Failure modes**

❌ Practising parry only when stakes are high → reinforces stress, not learning. ❌ Resetting after every miss → trains perfectionism, not recovery. ❌ Optimising builds during training → confounds signal ("was it timing or stats?").

**Bottom line**

You don't need perfect timing, harder enemies, or self-handicapping runs. You need controlled repetition, survivable mistakes, and permission to be imperfect for 10–15 minutes at a time. This is the kind of skill that compounds quietly once the pressure is removed.
