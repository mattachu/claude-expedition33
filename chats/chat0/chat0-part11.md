That's a really good thing to pause on, because AP flow is the hidden system that explains why some builds "feel smooth" and others feel clunky, even when stats look fine.

**1. What "AP flow" actually means (plain language)**

AP flow = how reliably a character can keep doing useful things every turn.

It's not just "how much AP do I have?" — it's: where does AP come from, when does it arrive, and what happens if something goes wrong?

A character with good AP flow rarely has a "dead turn", doesn't depend on perfect conditions, and can recover after mistakes. A character with bad AP flow runs out of AP after one big move or needs kills / crits / perfect timing.

**2. The four sources of AP (the mental model)**

- **A. Start-of-fight AP** — Energising Start, opening bonuses. Good for openers and setup turns; bad if relied on too heavily (it's gone after turn 1).
- **B. Action-triggered AP** — heal → AP, Break → AP, attack → AP. This is the best kind: repeatable, predictable, doesn't require success beyond "I did the thing."
- **C. Conditional AP** — on kill, on crit, on parry, on counter. Volatile: amazing when fights go well, disappears when things go badly.
- **D. Item-based AP** — Tint use, emergency recovery. Good as a safety valve, not a core engine.

**3. Why AP flow matters more than raw AP**

Two characters can both "generate AP" but feel totally different. +3 AP on kill is useless in a boss fight; +1 AP on heal works every turn. When we talk about AP flow, we're really asking: how often can this character expect to trigger their AP source in a normal fight?

**4. Your party's AP flow (character by character)**

**🟫 MONOCO — Reactive AP loop**

Where his AP comes from: Accelerating Heal → heal = Rush (more turns), Charging Tint → item = Gradient, Teamwork → flat, unconditional, normal turns (he's alive longer now).

What this means: Monoco's AP does not depend on kills, crits, or parries. If something goes wrong, his AP flow actually improves (because he heals more). How to play it: don't hoard AP on Monoco — use heals/items when needed, that is how he generates momentum.

**🟥 MAELLE — Break-driven AP**

Where her AP comes from: Fueling Break → Break = AP, natural high Speed → more turns, no kill dependency (after removing Dead Energy).

What this means: Maelle's AP flow is reliable in boss fights and strongest mid-fight. She ramps up as pressure builds. How to play it: don't force early nukes, spend AP aggressively during Break windows, it's fine if her first turn is light.

**🟦 LUNE — Heal + setup engine**

Where her AP comes from: Energising Heal → heal = AP, Protecting Heal → heal = Shell, Accelerating Heal → heal = Rush. One action gives survivability, AP, and tempo.

What this means: Lune's AP flow is strongest when damage has already happened and fights last more than 2 turns. She wants to heal even when no one is critical — a "small heal" can be an AP investment. Use Crystal Crush once AP is flowing, not before.

**🟨 SCIEL (when used) — Front-loaded AP**

Energising Start IV gives strong turns 1–2 but weak mid-fight AP. That's why she's an opener, not a mainstay.

**🟦 VERSO (later) — Execution-gated AP**

Crystal Crush costs AP and generates Break, not AP — but it indirectly fuels Maelle's AP via Fueling Break. The loop is: Lune heals → AP + Rush → Lune uses Crystal Crush → Break → Maelle triggers Break → AP → Monoco stabilises → more heals → more AP. That's a closed, forgiving AP loop. This is why Crystal Crush replaces Mayhem: Mayhem consumes AP; Crystal Crush enables AP elsewhere.

**6. One practical rule per character**

- Monoco: healing is not passive — it's how you generate momentum.
- Maelle: AP spikes during Break, not at the start.
- Lune: healing early is an investment, not a waste.
- Sciel: spend everything early or rotate out.
- Verso: AP flow comes from execution, not stats.
